## Design Context

### Users

South Asian singles and their social circles in NYC (and touring cities), discovered primarily via Instagram Reels and social shares. Context is mixed: commuter scrolling on a subway between stops, pre-show excitement on a Friday night, first-time visitors arriving from a viral clip. The job to be done is always the same: instantly understand what this show is, feel the pull of it, and buy a ticket (or tell someone who will). Secondary jobs: apply to be a contestant, join the Spice List email list.

### Brand Personality

**Sexy, spicy, unhinged.** The show is a cultural phenomenon that happens to be a comedy dating show. It is unhinged in the best way: real stakes, real chemistry, real audience chaos. But it is never cheap or sloppy. The warmth and the cultural roots keep it grounded. The energy is a Friday night out, not a startup pitch.

Three-word test for any new design decision: does this feel sexy, spicy, and unhinged? If it feels safe, corporate, or templated, redo it.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Movement, not marketing.** Design like you are announcing a cultural event worth making plans for, not selling a product. The show is so good it sells itself. The design's job is to make that obvious without being salesy.

**North star:** Would this be featured on Awwwards? Would someone screenshot this and send it to a designer friend? If not, keep going.

**Reference energy (not literal templates):**

- Spotify Wrapped: bold data-driven storytelling, unexpected use of color and motion, feels personal and cultural
- Apple product pages: every word and pixel earns its place, restraint creates impact
- Shopify Editions: editorial confidence, design as announcement not explanation
- Linear marketing: sharp, intentional, nothing generic anywhere on the page

**Unexpected over expected.** Conventional layouts are invisible. If a section layout exists on a thousand other sites, it belongs on none of them here. Break the grid with purpose. Use scale contrast, editorial tension, layered type.

**Conversion is the north star, disguised as art.** Every aesthetic choice must also move visitors toward buying tickets. Beauty is not decoration; it is the mechanism of conversion. If a section looks stunning but does not pull someone toward a CTA, redesign it.

### Design Principles

1. **Every pixel converts.** Beautiful and effective are not in tension; they are the same thing here. A section that does not move someone toward tickets is unfinished, no matter how it looks.

2. **The show sells itself: get out of the way.** Copy is minimal and confident. Never explain what the audience can feel. Replace descriptions with social proof, real reactions, real moments.

3. **Unexpected is the baseline.** Generic layouts are not a starting point to improve; they are disqualified. Asymmetry, editorial scale contrast, layered type, and broken grids are the default register.

4. **Warm and electric, never cool.** The palette is red, yellow, and warm off-white. Every new UI surface should feel warm and alive. Cool blues, corporate grays, and muted neutrals are antithetical to this brand.

5. **Culturally grounded, universally felt.** The South Asian identity is real and present: not as decoration, not as clipart, not as a pattern. It lives in the copy voice, the warmth, the spice. The design does not need to announce it.

### Anti-references (never look like these)

- Corporate / SaaS landing pages: no Inter font, no rounded hero cards, no "How It Works" three-column icon grids, no blue gradient CTAs
- Generic Desi aesthetics: no sitar clipart, excessive orange, paisley patterns, or "authentic cultural" visual cliches used as decoration
- Cheap comedy club: no brick wall textures, no Comic Sans energy, nothing that reads as open-mic night
- Dating app UI: no swipe cards, no pink/purple gradients, nothing that looks like Hinge or Bumble
